Seasonality Analysis
Before fitting any model, it is essential to understand whether monthly return patterns repeat predictably across the calendar year. These plots decompose each sector's return series by month and by frequency, and the Friedman test determines whether any apparent seasonal structure is statistically significant or just noise.

Stationarity & Correlation
ARIMA and GARCH models require stationary inputs. This section applies three unit-root tests (ADF, Phillips-Perron, KPSS) to confirm that monthly log returns are stationary, and uses ACF and PACF plots to identify the lag structure that will inform ARIMA order selection. Without this step, any fitted model would be spurious.
ACF: Autocorrelation by Sector
Autocorrelation at each lag for all five sectors. Most lags are within confidence bands, confirming near-white-noise monthly returns.

ACF II: Remaining Sectors
Mild significant lags appear in a few sectors. BIC-guided auto.arima penalizes over-parameterization, so these are not blindly included.

PACF: Partial Autocorrelation and AR Cues
PACF isolates direct lag effects. Mostly insignificant values guide auto.arima toward low-order models, avoiding overfitting.

Correlation Stability Check

Mean Model Fitting: ARIMA & Exponential Smoothing
ARIMA and exponential smoothing models are fitted to capture any predictable structure in the mean of monthly log returns. The key diagnostic is whether the residuals behave like white noise: if they do, the mean model has extracted all available linear signal and the remaining volatility clustering can be handed off to the GARCH stage.
ARIMA Residuals: Time Series
Residuals over time for each sector. Large spikes at crisis dates show heteroscedasticity that ARIMA cannot model, directly motivating GARCH.
Mean Models
Residual Histograms: Heavy Tails
Residuals show heavier tails than Gaussian. This leptokurtosis justifies using a Student-t distribution in the EGARCH variance equation.
Mean Models
SES & Holt Residuals
Smoothing model residuals mirror ARIMA residuals. The same crisis spikes confirm the variance structure is in the data, not the model.
Mean Models
Residual ACF: Whiteness Confirmed
All residual ACF lags fall within confidence bands. White noise in the mean is confirmed, clearing the way for GARCH on the squared residuals.

Volatility Modeling & Crisis Analysis
GARCH and EGARCH models are fitted to the ARIMA residuals to capture time-varying volatility. EGARCH is preferred because it allows negative shocks (bad news) to impact volatility more than positive shocks of equal size, a well-documented leverage effect in equity markets. Crisis periods are then labeled on the volatility series to quantify how each sector responds to systemic stress events.
Rolling Volatility: Five Sectors Over Time
12-month rolling vol for all sectors. Volatility clustering is clearly visible, with GFC, Taper, and COVID as the three dominant spikes.
Volatility & Crisis
GARCH vs EGARCH: Volatility Dynamics
EGARCH wins on BIC for all sectors. The difference is largest around crash events, where asymmetric leverage effect amplifies downside volatility beyond what GARCH models.

Crisis Volatility Profile: Color-Coded Events
Each crisis window colored distinctly. Bank sector leads during GFC; defensive sectors (Pharma, FMCG) show relative calm during COVID compared to cyclicals.

Crisis Comparison: Sector Sensitivity by Event
COVID and GFC dominate in absolute volatility. Demonetization hits domestic sectors hardest, showing that policy shocks have different transmission channels than global crises.

Volatility Forecasting
The final stage evaluates how well EGARCH(1,1) forecasts future volatility out-of-sample. A rolling one-step-ahead forecast is generated for each day in the 365-day test window, and the forecast is compared against 20-day realized volatility. RMSE, MAE, and MAPE quantify accuracy and allow direct comparison across sectors.
Forecast Setup: Rolling EGARCH Design
Rolling one-step-ahead EGARCH vs 20-day realized vol. Expanding window prevents look-ahead bias and mirrors real-world forecasting conditions.
